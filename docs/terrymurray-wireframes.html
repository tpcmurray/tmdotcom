Why Vibe Coding Will Eat Spec-Driven Development

February 14, 2026 · 6 min read

I've been watching something happen across our engineering teams that I think most people in leadership aren't paying attention to. The developers who ship the fastest aren't the ones writing the most detailed specs. They're the ones who've figured out how to think alongside an LLM.

The Spec Is a Crutch

For decades, we've treated the specification document as the single source of truth. And for good reason — when you're coordinating 20 developers across three time zones, you need a shared understanding of what's being built. But here's the thing: the spec was never the goal. The spec was a compression artifact — a lossy encoding of what someone actually wanted.

What if you could just... describe what you wanted, and get working software?

"The best code is the code you never had to write a spec for." — someone who's about to be proven right

I've been running an experiment with two teams at Verafin. One follows our standard spec-driven workflow. The other uses what I'm calling "vibe coding" — iterative, conversational development with an LLM, guided by intent rather than specification. The results after six weeks are hard to ignore.

The vibe coding team ships 3x faster and has comparable defect rates. The spec team produces more documentation. That's about it.

What This Means for Our Industry

I'm not saying specs are dead tomorrow. But I am saying that the ratio of "time spent specifying" to "time spent building" is about to invert. And teams that don't adapt will find themselves writing beautiful documents that describe software their competitors shipped last month.
